## 1. The bottleneck is us

*Judgment essay. Consensus hook, concede-then-pivot, bolded thesis on its own
line, a concrete PM-to-engineer failure chain, and a landing that redefines a
known idiom.*

> Everyone is talking about AI making development faster.
>
> And they're right, it does. But I don't think the real bottleneck was ever development.
>
> 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗯𝗼𝘁𝘁𝗹𝗲𝗻𝗲𝗰𝗸 𝗶𝘀 𝘂𝘀.
>
> More specifically: our presence, our judgment, and our willingness to deliberately curate what goes in and what comes out of every AI-driven workflow.
>
> Here's what I mean: A PM uses AI to write a ticket. If that artifact wasn't properly verified, shaped, owned, this artifact going downstream is hollow or misleading.
>
> Remember that AI loves to fill the gaps to look robust. It looks like a spec, smells like a spec but it doesn't carry the intent behind the problem.
>
> So AI on the other end builds something that is coherent but meaningfully off.
>
> This is the part we keep glossing over in the conversation about agentic workflows.
>
> We talk about which tools to use, how to chain models, how to automate reviews but the quality of everything the machine produces is directly proportional to the quality of what the human put in.
>
> And how critically the human is evaluating what comes out.
>
> 𝗚𝗮𝗿𝗯𝗮𝗴𝗲 𝗶𝗻, 𝗴𝗮𝗿𝗯𝗮𝗴𝗲 𝗼𝘂𝘁.
>
> Except now the garbage isn't bad code. It's the absence of human curation.

## 3. Critical thinking

concede-then-pivot, explicit self-implication, second-person accountability, and

> Recently, I have been thinking a lot about the role of AI in our lives as software engineers and it feels as if we are navigating a pandemic with no clear solution...
>
> It is already a cliché to say that you should not delegate your critical thinking to a machine, but I cannot emphasize this enough.
>
> Critical thinking is what makes us human and - ultimately - this is what we are paid to do.
>
> When you delegate the entire process, including the conceptualization of the problem, you lose the ability to judge if the solution provided is actually optimal.
>
> Sometimes, "good enough" is good enough. Not every task requires deep, systematic thinking. However, there is a vast universe of problems where an AI will simply do whatever it takes to finish the task.
>
> In those moments, you must be the driving force behind the solution, not a passive observer.
>
> For "vibe-coded" applications where code throughput is the only thing that matters, AI is incredible.
>
> But in a standard development lifecycle, failing to do your work means you are offloading the mental weight of your problem onto the person reviewing it. At that point, you become a burden.
>
> If you have built a personal "Jarvis" that truly automates your work, that's awesome!
>
> But for the majority of people, this reliance is just lack of standards.
>
> This is not an anti-AI manifesto. It is a warning to anyone - including myself - putting their career in the hands of a fancy autocomplete machine.
>
> It does not matter who wrote the code: you, an AI, or your cousin. At the end of the day, it is 𝘆𝗼𝘂𝗿 liability.
>
> We are paid to solve problems effectively. Effectively means ensuring that what we are doing now is not going to come back to bite us in a few weeks.
>
> When the only thing that matters is how fast you can move, be cautious.
>
> 𝗬𝗼𝘂 𝗰𝗼𝘂𝗹𝗱 𝗯𝗲 𝗺𝗼𝘃𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝗮𝘁 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝘀𝗽𝗲𝗲𝗱 𝗼𝗳 𝗮 𝗙𝗲𝗿𝗿𝗮𝗿𝗶 𝗱𝗶𝗿𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗹𝘆 𝗶𝗻𝘁𝗼 𝗮 𝗽𝗶𝘁.

>

>
> 𝗡𝗼𝗽𝗲! None of those.

>

>
> But there's a different kind of mastery, the kind that comes with discipline: simplicity.
>
> "𝘓𝘦𝘢𝘳𝘯 𝘵𝘩𝘦 𝘵𝘦𝘤𝘩𝘯𝘪𝘲𝘶𝘦, 𝘮𝘢𝘴𝘵𝘦𝘳 𝘵𝘩𝘦 𝘵𝘦𝘤𝘩𝘯𝘪𝘲𝘶𝘦, 𝘧𝘰𝘳𝘨𝘦𝘵 𝘵𝘩𝘦 𝘵𝘦𝘤𝘩𝘯𝘪𝘲𝘶𝘦."
>
> Learning how to build complex software is cool, but learning how to keep things simple is 𝙠𝙞𝙡𝙡𝙚𝙧.
>
> Think with me, what gives the best ROI?
>
> - Solution A: 5 points of effort, covers 80% of cases.
> - Solution B: 80 points of effort, covers 92% of cases.
>
> Most of the time, enough is enough. That first 80% is all you need to get moving.
>
> As we get more senior, we realize a lot of our appetite for complexity comes from the anxiety of solving problems that will likely never happen. Ghost optimizations for ghost problems.
>
> That's also where so much pain comes from, especially when abstractions start leaking, but that's a whole other can of worms.
>
> Here's a rule of thumb I try to never forget:
>
> Start simple, and small. Solve one specific problem at a time, then incrementally evolve your solution as the problem evolves.
>
> Simplicity is your best defense against software rot.
>
> In other words, simplicity will protect you from faulty abstractions, bugs, and maintenance hell.

## 8. Websocket mode

*News reaction. Links back to his own earlier post, explains the mechanism
rather than the announcement, works the numbers, and ends with a genuinely open
question to the audience.*

> OpenAI just launched a breakthrough that could slash the latency of our AI agents nearly in half.
>
> One of my first posts here was me 𝚛̶𝚊̶𝚗̶𝚝̶𝚒̶𝚗̶𝚐̶ complaining about how MCP servers are stateless. In that protocol, every single interaction requires your entire context to round-trip back and forth, regardless of size or relevance. If you are curious about the technical implications, check out that post here:
>
> https://lnkd.in/dxMFHVYB
>
> Yesterday, OpenAI released what they are calling "Websocket Mode."
>
> As the name suggests, we can now interact with their API using a persistent websocket connection. But the real meat of the update is the introduction of 𝘀𝘁𝗮𝘁𝗲𝗳𝘂𝗹 𝗰𝗼𝗻𝗻𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻𝘀.
>
> By using this mode, the service now maintains the state of the most recent response in an in-memory cache layer.
>
> With that, we can achieve significantly faster responses because the API no longer needs to fetch your context from the database for every request. The service already has everything it needs to proceed.
>
> By maintaining this in-memory state across requests, OpenAI promises response times that are 20% to 40% faster for agentic systems.
>
> 𝗘𝗺𝗽𝗵𝗮𝘀𝗶𝘀 𝗶𝗻 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗮𝗴𝗲𝗻𝘁𝗶𝗰 𝘀𝘆𝘀𝘁𝗲𝗺, becasuse if you're building your system on top of imperative LLM calls, there's no much overhead to optimize, right? (𝘩𝘰𝘯𝘦𝘴𝘵 𝘲𝘶𝘦𝘴𝘵𝘪𝘰𝘯)
>
> Imagine, for every tool call an agent makes, there is a new request to the LLM and a corresponding access to the disk. If your agent performs 20 tool calls, you are looking at 20 separate database reads.
>
> If we can keep all of that context close to the process and skip those reads entirely, it will certainly decrease the overall latency of the output in... maybe... 40%.
>
> Check the Ref: https://lnkd.in/dn7KSNyn

## 9. Lodash isEmpty

*War story, short form. Naive expectation, the wall with an emotional beat, the
doc quote as root cause, a numbered breakdown, and a casual sign-off.*

> A couple of weeks ago, I had to write a function that, given an arbitrary object payload, would process any properties that were empty. Simple and straightforward, I thought.
>
> The first thing that came to my mind was, "I'm going to loop over the properties and check if their values are empty using 𝘭𝘰𝘥𝘢𝘴𝘩.𝘪𝘴𝘌𝘮𝘱𝘵𝘺()."
>
> It sounded like the perfect plan. Wrote the test cases, built the function, and wait… wtf?!
>
> "Why are numbers and booleans being processed?!"
>
> I thought this function was simple enough to be flawless in one shot. Well, the culprit was Lodash. I never would have expected this, but their docs are clear. Their definition of empty is:
>
> "Objects are considered empty if they have no own enumerable string keyed properties."
>
> Specifically, that means:
>
> 1. 𝗢𝘄𝗻 𝗽𝗿𝗼𝗽𝗲𝗿𝘁𝗶𝗲𝘀: Properties defined directly on the object itself, not inherited from its prototype.
> 2. 𝗘𝗻𝘂𝗺𝗲𝗿𝗮𝗯𝗹𝗲: Properties that can be looped over.
> 3. 𝗦𝘁𝗿𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝗸𝗲𝘆𝗲𝗱: Properties where the key is a string, as opposed to a 𝘚𝘺𝘮𝘣𝘰𝘭.
>
> Because of this, a bunch of primitives are considered "empty" by default from Lodash's perspective.
>
> That is all for today, folks. Next time, take care when using those magical methods. See you in the next one! 👋🏻

## 10. react-email and the theme

*The canonical war story, full length. Every beat of the archetype is present:
in-group hook, naive expectation, the constraint, refusal to proceed without
understanding, the stat with a footnote, turning the question to the reader, the
rejected easy paths, the Kent Beck hinge, the parallel payoff list, the verdict.*

> Only those who have felt the pain of building email templates will truly understand. This week, I had to move our tech stack one step back so we could move two steps forward.
>
> I was introducing react-email to our codebase. An excellent library created by Resend that empowers engineers to build email templates using React and Tailwind in its pure form.
>
> In our current stack, we use Tailwind as our main styling tech and we use a Theme that serves as the foundation for our design system and web app.
>
> Naturally, I expected that our email templates could derive from that same theme. Since both our web app and react-email uses React and Tailwind, sharing the theme makes coding emails exactly like building a new application page.
>
> That sounds perfect, right? It would be, except if react-email does not support our current method of defining themes.
>
> The current form of defining themes in Tailwind v4 is defining your tokens using CSS variables, such as --𝗰𝗼𝗹𝗼𝗿-𝗯𝗹𝗮𝗰𝗸: #𝟬𝟬𝟬, that's how we do. However, the only way to use a theme in react-email is by defining it as a JavaScript object, which is the legacy method used in Tailwind v3.
>
> Before moving forward, I felt I couldn't write another line of code without understanding why a top-tier email library had such a significant constraint. After a full day of research and several proofs of concept, I found something that makes sense.
>
> More than 50% of email clients today do not support CSS variables. This includes major players like Gmail, Outlook, and Yahoo [1]. There is nothing I can do to change that reality.
>
> Faced with these facts, what would you do?
>
> The higher your seniority, more difficult decisions you will have to make. For these choices, you have to dive deep into the technical limitations and form an opinion after carefully reflecting on the trade-offs.
>
> I considered taking the easy path: duplicating the theme as a JS object, inlining styles manually, or abandoning react-email entirely.
>
> However, I remembered the words of Kent Beck:
>
> "𝙁𝙤𝙧 𝙚𝙖𝙘𝙝 𝙙𝙚𝙨𝙞𝙧𝙚𝙙 𝙘𝙝𝙖𝙣𝙜𝙚, 𝙢𝙖𝙠𝙚 𝙩𝙝𝙚 𝙘𝙝𝙖𝙣𝙜𝙚 𝙚𝙖𝙨𝙮 (𝙬𝙖𝙧𝙣𝙞𝙣𝙜, 𝙩𝙝𝙞𝙨 𝙢𝙖𝙮 𝙗𝙚 𝙝𝙖𝙧𝙙), 𝙩𝙝𝙚𝙣 𝙢𝙖𝙠𝙚 𝙩𝙝𝙚 𝙚𝙖𝙨𝙮 𝙘𝙝𝙖𝙣𝙜𝙚."
>
> After reflecting on the trade-offs, I decided that the hard path was the right move.
>
> I took on the difficult task of migrating our theme to a format compatible with both systems. This was not straightforward, especially when considering presets, plugins. It also required ensuring 100% coverage with zero visual regressions.
>
> This effort was necessary to make the addition of new technology easy. By doing the hard work now, we can perform the easy change of seamlessly integrating react-email into our app moving forward.
>
> For engineers, it means better DX and quality of life.
> For design, it ensures high fidelity.
> For the product, it means faster deliveries.
>

## 11. Production scripts

*Checklist. Framed as thinking out loud, bold section headers, three parallel
dash-bullets each, one line of synthesis at the end, and an open invitation.*

> I was thinking about the amount of things we should take care before running a script on production. Here are the things I come up with:
>
> 𝗜𝗱𝗲𝗺𝗽𝗼𝘁𝗲𝗻𝗰𝘆
> - No duplicate updates or corrupted data
> - Ensure your script can run twice safely
> - Modified records stay modified once
>
> 𝗟𝗮𝘁𝗲𝗻𝗰𝘆
> - Consider production scale before running
> - Test your queries with real data volumes
> - Use database indexes to speed things up
>
> 𝗠𝗲𝗺𝗼𝗿𝘆 𝗖𝗼𝗻𝘀𝘂𝗺𝗽𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻
> - Process data in chunks, not all at once
> - Don't pull everything into memory at once
> - Use batching strategies as cursors or sliding windows
>
> 𝗘𝗱𝗴𝗲 𝗖𝗮𝘀𝗲𝘀
> - Account for the weird stuff that always exists
> - Sample production data before running
> - Test against different scenarios
>
> 𝗢𝗯𝘀𝗲𝗿𝘃𝗮𝗯𝗶𝗹𝗶𝘁𝘆
> - Logs, logs, and more logs
> - Track every step your script takes
> - Make debugging possible when things break
>
> 𝗕𝗔𝗖𝗞𝗨𝗣 ‼️
> - If none of the above prevent an incident, have a rollback plan ready
> - A pro tip is: always run your script right after the latest backup
>
